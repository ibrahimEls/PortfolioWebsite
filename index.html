About/CV

Physics for AI,

I'm a physicist and machine learning researcher working at the intersection of fundamental science and artificial intelligence. My research spans using physics-inspired theory to understand neural network behavior, building foundation models that transfer across scientific domains, and developing AI tools that automate scientific discovery.

Currently a PhD candidate at the University of Toronto and doctoral researcher at Berkeley Lab (NERSC), I bring experience from CERN particle physics, ExxonMobil geophysics, and foundational ML theory work at UIUC.

Research

Research Interests

My work lives at the boundary between physics and machine learning, moving in both directions: using physics to build better AI, and using AI to do better physics.

Physics for AI

Physics Data for Understanding ML

Leveraging the unique properties of physics datasets—where we have access to simulators, known symmetries, and controllable data generation—to probe and understand the internal mechanisms of deep neural networks.

Physics for AI

Physics-Inspired Theory for Scaling Laws

Using tools from statistical mechanics and effective field theory to predict how neural network ensembles behave, deriving scaling laws for uncertainty quantification without exhaustive training.

AI for Physics

Automating Scientific Model Building

Developing ML methods—such as contrastive normalizing flows—that can perform robust parameter estimation and model inference in the presence of systematic uncertainties, automating what has traditionally required deep domain expertise.

AI for Physics

Cross-Domain Foundation Models

Building foundation models for scientific point clouds and graphs that transfer knowledge across particle physics, cosmology, and molecular dynamics—enabling a single model to serve multiple scientific domains.
